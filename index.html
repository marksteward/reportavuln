You've found a nasty vulnerability and want to do the right thing.


Now what?

What

Doing the right thing basically means telling those responsible for the service or product that it is vulnerable and is putting users at risk.

Non Disclosure

Let them know, then sit back, relax and forget about it.

The idea here is that you do not disclose the vulernability to the public. Instead, you notify the owner, provide them with the relevant details then let them get on with it. Unfortunately, a lot of vendors and services do not take responsiblity for such security problems and could simply ignore your notification without ever fixing the problem. This means users remain at risk. The other form of non disclosure is simply not to inform the vendor and to keep the details to yourself (or exploit them in some way). However, this isn't really doing the right thing, so won't be considered here.

Responsible Disclosure

Let them know, but give them a time frame in which to fix the issue. Then go public.

Instead of just notifying them and forgetting about it, you can give them a time frame in which they need to fix the issue before you go public with the vulnerability. This puts pressure on the vendor to actually fix it, otherwise they could face bad publicity and further ramifications of the details going public. A time frame of one or two months should probably do, but you might need to adjust it depending on the severity of the problem.

The danger here is that they might still ignore you which means you either have to give in or go public. If you go public, you might be directly putting the users at risk as anyone can now use that vulnerability. Having said that, it isn't your responsibility and you did try. Maybe.

Full Disclosure

No warning. Go public.

Here the details are published in full without warning to the vendor or service. This immediately exposes users to risk but could result in a swift response by the vendor.

You'll probably want to go with responsible disclosure.

Who

Who ya gonna call? Hostmasters!

Disclosure Policy

Read it.

If the vendor or service has a disclosure policy, it should contain details on how to notify them of vulnerabilities. If they have a bug bounty program, even better. You might even get paid for reporting the issue.

Send an email

security@domain

If their website doesn't explicitly mention how to notifying them of security issues, then you might want to try sending them an email. RFC 2142 suggests the use of security@domain but if that doesn't work you can also try hostmaster@domain and webmaster@domain.

Technical Support

Be careful.

Finally, you could try to notifying them through their usual support channel. This might be a contact form on their website or a specific technical support email address. The danger here is that you don't know who will have access to your notification and it could get into the wrong hands. Ideally you would contact a security team directly, but this is generally not an option for most support channels.

How

People don't take bad news too well. Companies are no different.

Stay anonymous

Temporary email and TOR

There have been a number of cases recently where a security researcher has had threatening legal letters and even a visit from the police, all for just trying to do the right thing. Telling a company that their software is broken can potentially upset them. Rather than take responsibility for the problem, they might try to blame you. They might claim you were attempting to "hack" them. They might get lawyers involved. Or the police. If you want to protect yourself from potential hassle, you could consider using TOR to access a temporary email service such as 10 Minute Mail.

Disclosure Policy

Read it.

If they have a disclosure policy then it suggests they may have at least thought about the possibility of having to handle a vulnerability notification. Hopefully they will accept the notification with gratitude and will take it seriously. Or at least give you a realistic time frame. You may even feel comfortable sending the notification as yourself so they can get back to you if required.

Be yourself

They might say thank you.

You final choice is to notify them without hiding your identity. This would allow them to get back to you to request further details, to tell you it is fixed, or perhaps to say thank you. They might also give you credit.

The Disclosure

The time has finally come...

If you chose to disclose the vulnerability responsibly and gave them a time frame in which to fix the problem, it is entirely possible that for one reason or another they won't have fixed the issue on time. They might even ask you for more time. But at some point you might just decide to go public. As with the notification, you have the choice of doing it anonymously or using your identity. You could contact a major IT news website and give them the details, or you could post to the Full Disclosure Mailing List.

Help

If you're still unsure...

Try a CERT

Computer Emergency Response Team

Some CERTs will take vulnerability notifications and will contact the vendor or service on your behalf. For example, the US CERT provides a form for you to submit vulnerability reports. The Wikipedia CERT page has a list of CERTs on it.

For sale

Zero Day Initiative

The other alternative is to try selling your vulnerability to a program like TippingPoint's Zero Day Initiative. For example, TippingPoint might pay you for a vulnerability and then use it for its own commercial benefit by developing IPS protection filters which are made available to its customers. It will then also coordinate with the vendor to get the problem fixed. More details can be found here.
